Part I · Money, Returns, And Risk

Risk And Downside Losses

Can two investments reach the same destination while giving their owners completely different experiences?

Beginner levelBuilds on compoundingInteractive drawdown laboratory
The opening question

Both paths end at $103. Are they equally risky?

Investor A starts with $100 and sees wealth move gently through $101 and $102 before reaching $103. Investor B also starts with $100 and finishes at $103, but first rises to $120, crashes to $80, and then recovers. Their final gains are equal. Their paths are not.

Path A · steady
$100 → $101 → $102 → $103

Small changes, no fall from a previous peak, and a calm experience.

Path B · turbulent
$100 → $120 → $80 → $103

A one-third loss from the $120 peak occurs before the same final wealth is reached.

If we inspect only starting and ending wealth, the paths look equivalent. A person living through Path B may face panic, a margin call, a forced sale, or a spending need while wealth is depressed. Risk metrics summarize aspects of the journey that final return cannot show.

One idea to rememberReturn describes reward; risk describes uncertainty and harmful paths. No single number captures every form of risk, so each metric must be tied to the question it answers.
Build the vocabulary

Risk is not only one thing

Uncertainty means that more than one future outcome is possible. Risk adds concern about consequences, especially losses or failure to meet an objective. In finance, the word is often used for measurable variability, but an investor may also care about liquidity, inflation, default, currency, legal, operational, and model risk.

Volatility measures how widely returns vary around their average. Both positive and negative surprises contribute. A large positive day raises measured volatility even though the investor gained money. Volatility is useful because unstable returns make future wealth less predictable, but it does not distinguish welcome upside from painful downside.

Downside risk focuses on unfavorable outcomes. The paper uses this phrase for the danger of losses during adverse market regimes. It is not the same as a company-level “downside beta” factor. In this course, context will always state whether downside refers to negative returns, drawdowns, expected shortfall, or the paper's broader goal.

A peak is the highest wealth reached so far. A drawdown is the percentage decline from that running peak to current wealth. Maximum drawdown, abbreviated MDD, is the worst such decline in the measured path. It remembers order: a loss before a peak and the same loss after a peak can produce different drawdown histories.

A tail loss is an unusually bad outcome in the lower part of the return distribution. Expected shortfall averages returns in a specified worst fraction, such as the worst 5%. It asks how bad returns were after entering the tail, not merely where the tail threshold begins.

Every risk statement also needs a horizon. Daily volatility, annual wealth drawdown, and a ten-year probability of failing to meet spending needs are different objects. An asset may look stable from day to day yet lose purchasing power slowly, or look volatile daily while remaining suitable for a long-horizon investor. This project measures daily return paths and annualizes selected summaries; it does not convert them into a complete personal risk assessment.

Variability

Volatility treats large moves in either direction as dispersion.

Depth

Maximum drawdown measures the deepest peak-to-trough loss.

Tail

Worked example one

Same average, different volatility

Compare four daily returns. Series A is 0%, 0%, 0%, 0%. Series B is +2%, −2%, +2%, −2%. Both have an arithmetic average of 0%. Series A never moves, while Series B repeatedly moves two percentage points away from the average. Its volatility is positive.

The first step is to subtract the average return from each observation. These differences are called deviations. Squaring them prevents positive and negative deviations from cancelling and places more weight on large moves. Average the squared deviations to obtain a variance, then take the square root to return to return units.

s= ∑t=1n(rt−r¯)2 n−1
SymbolMeaning
rtReturn observation at index t
r¯Arithmetic average of the measured returns

The frozen project uses sample standard deviation with denominator n−1, recorded as volatility_ddof = 1. Daily volatility is multiplied by the square root of 252 to report an annualized value. This scaling is a convention based on approximately 252 trading periods per year and assumptions about aggregation; it does not guarantee that tomorrow behaves independently like yesterday.

The square-root-of-time rule is exact only under restrictive behavior such as independent returns with stable variance. Financial returns can cluster: calm days tend to follow calm periods and turbulent days can arrive together. The paper and project still use the conventional annualization because comparable reporting requires a declared rule. Honest use means naming the convention and avoiding the claim that annualization has removed time dependence.

A common failure: “high volatility means bad return”

Volatility measures dispersion, not direction. A sequence of unexpectedly large gains can have high volatility. Conversely, a steady small loss can have low volatility while consistently destroying wealth. This is one reason the paper combines risk and return features rather than labeling regimes from volatility alone in the Jump Model.

When the default shock is −20%, wealth falls from the $105 peak to $84. Drawdown is $84 divided by $105 minus one, exactly −20%. A 15% recovery acts on $84, not on the old peak, so it reaches $96.60. Recovering fully from a 20% loss requires a 25% gain because $84 must gain $21 to return to $105.

Maximum drawdown records depth but not duration. One portfolio may recover from a 20% drawdown in a week; another may remain below its peak for years. Both can report the same MDD. The paper's metric table emphasizes depth, while its cumulative paths help the reader see timing and recovery. A future research question about investor endurance would need an explicit duration or time-under-water metric rather than silently reading duration into MDD.

A common failure: measuring loss from the original $100

At the shock, wealth is $84, which is 16% below the original $100. Maximum drawdown is nevertheless 20% because the relevant reference is the running $105 peak. Drawdown answers “how far below the best wealth seen so far?” It does not answer “how far below the initial deposit?”

Downside-only variability

Downside deviation ignores positive returns

The Jump Model uses downside deviation as a feature. First replace every positive excess return with zero while keeping negative excess returns. Square those downside observations, calculate an average—with exponential weights in the real feature—and take a square root. Large negative returns receive more influence because they are squared.

DD= E[R21{R<0}]

R is excess return. The indicator 1{R<0} equals one for a negative observation and zero otherwise. The expectation E means an average under the chosen weighting scheme. Chapter 8 will explain exponential weighting and the 10-day half-life.

Downside deviation is not drawdown. It summarizes negative return magnitudes without tracking a running wealth peak. Two return sequences with the same negative values in different order can have the same downside deviation but different drawdown paths. Each metric preserves different information.

Reward relative to risk

Sharpe, Sortino, and Calmar answer different questions

A raw return does not reveal how unstable or painful the path was. A risk-adjusted metric places a reward measure over a risk measure. Higher values are usually preferred, but only when numerator, denominator, period, sample, and cash convention are identical.

Sharpe ratio

Average excess return divided by strategy-return volatility. It penalizes upside and downside variability.

Sortino ratio

Average excess return divided by downside deviation. It focuses the denominator on harmful moves.

Calmar ratio

Annualized excess reward divided by the absolute maximum drawdown. It uses the worst wealth-path decline.

Sharpe= 252 mean(rstrategy−rcash) std(rstrategy)

This is the project's exact frozen Sharpe definition. Some sources use the standard deviation of excess returns instead. The two may be close when cash changes slowly, but they are not definitionally identical. A replication must match the chosen convention rather than selecting whichever version gives a better result.

A ratio can become unstable when its denominator is near zero. Calmar is undefined when no negative drawdown exists under the project convention. Sortino is missing when downside deviation is zero. A high ratio based on very few observations may be unreliable. Metrics require validation, sufficient sample size, and uncertainty analysis; ranking rounded values is not enough for a scientific claim.

The lower tail

Expected shortfall looks beyond a cutoff

Sort daily strategy returns from worst to best. The 5% quantile marks a threshold near the worst twentieth of observations. Expected shortfall at 5% averages returns at or below that threshold. If the worst observations are −10%, −6%, and −5%, their average says more about tail severity than merely reporting that the cutoff is −5%.

Expected shortfall is sensitive to sample size and extreme observations. With few observations, “worst 5%” contains very few days. Historical expected shortfall also describes the observed sample; it is not a guarantee that future losses cannot be worse. The paper reports this measure as one view of downside protection, not as a maximum possible loss.

Quantile conventions can also differ when the desired percentile lies between observed ranks. Software may interpolate, choose a neighboring order statistic, or include a different number of tied observations. The frozen implementation first calculates the pandas 5% threshold and then averages every strategy return at or below it. That exact rule must be held constant across models; changing interpolation after seeing results would be another hidden degree of freedom.

Paper connection

Downside-risk reduction is the paper's primary motivation

The abstract and Section 1 of Shu, Yu, and Mulvey (2024), arXiv:2402.05272v3 frame the strategy as a way to reduce exposure during anticipated unfavorable regimes. Section 2 describes deep historical losses in the three equity indices. Section 3.4.1 chooses downside deviation and Sortino features because investors care differently about downside losses and upside gains.

Table 4 reports return, volatility, Sharpe, maximum drawdown, Calmar, expected shortfall, turnover, and leverage for buy-and-hold, HMM, and JM strategies. The paper's argument is a pattern across several metrics and markets, not one favorable cell. Figures 5 and 6 connect inferred bear regimes to cumulative excess-return paths and reveal both protected drawdowns and signal latency.

Paper interpretationThe authors report that JM-guided switching improves downside and risk-adjusted metrics relative to their HMM and buy-and-hold comparisons. This is an empirical finding under their data and protocol, not a mathematical consequence of adding a jump penalty.
Project connection

Where the frozen metrics are computed

The active implementation is performance_metrics() in src/adaptive_jump/backtest.py. It compounds strategy returns into wealth, calculates the running peak and drawdown, annualizes sample volatility, subtracts aligned cash in the Sharpe numerator, and computes the remaining frozen metrics.

wealth = cumprod(1.0 + strategy_return)
peaks = maximum_accumulate([1.0, *wealth])
drawdowns = [1.0, *wealth] / peaks - 1.0
maximum_drawdown = min(drawdowns)

The exact conventions are locked under [metrics] in research.toml. The independent run verifier reloads each daily trade path and recomputes metrics. The English generated report displays those verified results. This chapter deliberately contains no copied project result table.

Evidence and limits

A metric is a lens, not a verdict

Established here

Return paths with the same endpoint can have different volatility, drawdown, downside deviation, and tail behavior.

Not established

No toy path demonstrates that HMM or JM switching reduces real risk, survives costs, or generalizes beyond its sample.

Scientific boundaryThe interactive path is invented and user-controlled. It teaches metric mechanics only. Changing sliders until a path looks attractive would be visual cherry-picking, not research.

Metrics also omit experiences that may matter: recovery time, sequence risk around withdrawals, intraday losses hidden by daily closes, taxes, and capacity. A protocol must choose metrics before inspecting the result and report unfavorable values rather than switching definitions afterward.

Metric comparisons also require a common sample. If one model has missing early signals and is measured only during a later calm period, comparing its Sharpe with a buy-and-hold value measured through an earlier crisis confounds model and dates. The v7 protocol intersects complete metric rows for every model within each market and delay before calculating the table. This does not guarantee a successful replication, but it makes the comparison population coherent.

Calculation: recover from a 25% drawdown

Wealth falls from $100 to $75. Returning to $100 requires a $25 gain on a $75 base: 25 / 75 = 33.33%. The recovery percentage is larger than the loss percentage.

Error diagnosis: “lower volatility proves better timing”

A strategy can lower volatility simply by holding cash more often. To evaluate timing, compare return, risk, leverage, costs, and suitable baselines on the same dates. Lower exposure alone is not proof of predictive or identification skill.

Cumulative recall from Chapters 1–2

The held position controls simple strategy return. Market return must include the declared distribution treatment; cash must be aligned to the same period; compounded returns create the wealth path from which drawdown is measured.

Advisor explanation

“The paper does not define success only as higher return. Its motivation is downside protection, so I must compare volatility, maximum drawdown, expected shortfall, and risk-adjusted reward under one frozen metric convention.”

How to explain this to your advisor

Short version: “Ending return hides the path. Volatility measures dispersion, drawdown measures the deepest fall from a previous peak, and expected shortfall measures average tail severity. The paper evaluates all three.”

Important qualification: “Risk reduction can come from lower market exposure rather than superior timing, so leverage, cash return, costs, and reward metrics must be reported together.”
