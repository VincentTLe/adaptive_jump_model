\begin{abstract}
Shu, Yu, and Mulvey use a statistical Jump Model (JM) to identify persistent
favorable and unfavorable equity-market regimes. Their strategy holds the
market in the favorable state and local cash in the unfavorable state. On
their licensed 1990--2023 sample, the JM beats both buy-and-hold and a Gaussian
hidden Markov model (HMM) on Sharpe ratio and maximum drawdown in the United
States, Germany, and Japan. We reconstruct their past-only economic test on
public proxy series through 2023 and ask whether JM persistence can be improved
without losing exact decoding.

The mathematical extension replaces the fixed jump cost with a bounded,
loss-gap-dependent transition cost. Exact dynamic programming is retained,
the fixed JM is nested at \(\beta=0\), and a two-state recursion identifies a
branch in which arrival-day evidence is used twice. Lagging the evidence removes
that direct same-day reuse; a pair-balanced rule preserves the average cost of
opposite directions. We also test five simpler controls: fixed
\(\lambda=50\), downside-deviation-only features, two-observation confirmation,
a matured-return-aware training loss, and robust cityblock loss.

The public proxy does not reproduce the parent ordering. Fixed JM beats both
same-sample controls in zero of three markets, and the frozen arrival-adaptive
trade-off rule is officially \texttt{not\_supported}. The largest local result
is downside deviation (DD) alone: US net Sharpe is 0.91, versus 0.57 for
fixed JM, 0.65 for HMM, and 0.51 for buy-and-hold. A prespecified control
then multiplies DD observation loss by three while keeping the raw lambda grid
fixed. It retains a positive Sharpe improvement over fixed JM in all three
markets but beats both controls only in the US. This favors feature removal
over raw loss scale as the stronger explanation, although finite-grid binding
and one-state fits prevent a clean feature-selection claim. Extending the
proxies to 2026 and opening a selection-clean 2024-2026 window once leaves the
US edge intact on the full walk-forward but cannot confirm it on the short
post-selection bull. These repeatedly inspected results are development
evidence, not a claim of robust, generalizable, or live profit.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} market regimes; statistical jump models; hidden
Markov models; downside risk; transition penalties; walk-forward validation.

\section{Introduction}

Equity markets pass through persistent periods with different return and risk
characteristics \citep{pagan2003,schwert1989}. This motivates a simple
investment question. If an unfavorable period can be recognized soon enough,
moving from equity to cash can reduce downside risk. If the signal follows daily
noise, however, repeated exits and re-entries create whipsaw and trading costs;
if the signal is too persistent, it reacts late. We call this the
latency--whipsaw trade-off.

The 0/1 strategy makes the quality of a regime signal visible. It holds 100\%
equity in a favorable state and 100\% local cash in an unfavorable state.
There is no portfolio optimizer to hide the signal's economic effect. HMM-based
versions of this strategy provide a standard likelihood-based comparator
\citep{bulla2011}. A Gaussian HMM treats the regime as a latent Markov chain and
estimates conditional return distributions and transition probabilities. A
statistical JM is closer to clustering: observations are assigned to fitted
centers, but every adjacent state change pays an explicit cost
\citep{bemporad2018,nystrup2020persistent}.

Shu, Yu, and Mulvey connect this persistence mechanism to a tradable test
\citep{shu2024}. They fit two-state HMMs and JMs using only past observations,
select smoothing and jump penalties monthly with the preceding eight years of
delayed and costed strategy Sharpe, and then trade with a one-day delay and a
10-basis-point one-way cost. Their main result is not merely that JM clusters
look smoother. On licensed 1990--2023 data, the resulting JM strategy improves
Sharpe and maximum drawdown relative to both HMM and \BH{} in all three
markets.

The fixed JM nevertheless charges the same \(\lambda\) for every possible
switch. A transition supported by a large state-loss gap pays exactly the same
cost as one supported by almost no gap. This suggests a direct extension:
retain the fixed barrier by default but discount it when past-only fit evidence
favors the destination. The first question is mathematical: can this be done
without losing exact dynamic programming, a training-only scale, or the fixed
JM special case? The second is economic: can one prespecified JM variant beat
both same-sample \BH{} and HMM under identical timing and costs?

This study makes three bounded contributions. First, it formulates three
loss-gap-dependent transition rules and derives their exact dynamic programs,
fixed-JM nesting, two-state recursion, and pair-balance identity. Second, it
tests five small alternatives to transition adaptation under the same causal
economic protocol. Third, it documents where the mechanisms help and fail on
three public proxy samples. No one tested variant wins in all three markets;
the DD loss-scale control narrows, but does not remove, the interpretation
problem. We therefore treat all market results as exploratory development
evidence. Here ``causal'' means past-only information timing, not
treatment-effect identification.

The JM features follow Shu et al. \citep{shu2024}. They draw on the classical

\[

(r^x_t)^2\ind\{r^x_t<0\}\right]},
\qquad
Sortino_h(t)=\frac{\operatorname{EWM}_h[r^x_t]}{DD_h(t)}.
\]
Here DD always means downside deviation; MDD below means maximum drawdown.
The feature identities and half-lives follow Shu et al.; pandas exponential
weights with \texttt{adjust=True} and \texttt{ignore\_na=False}, zero-DD
missing values, and no clipping are disclosed repository conventions. At each
JM refit, all used

\subsection{Walk-forward fitting and model selection}

The two-state HMM observes daily log return. It is refit every day on the
trailing 3,000 finite observations. Ten seeds, 0 through 9, initialize
conditional means with KMeans++; each KMeans call uses
\texttt{n\_init=10}. The accepted fit has the highest finite log-likelihood.
The implementation is \texttt{hmmlearn} 0.3.3 with diagonal covariance,
minimum covariance \(10^{-3}\), covariance prior 0.01, unit start and
transition priors, at most 1,000 iterations, and a strict absolute likelihood
increment tolerance of \(10^{-6}\). The lower-variance state is favorable. The
terminal Viterbi state is the online state, and a causal trailing mean labels
the state unfavorable only when it exceeds 0.5. Shu et al. publish the ten
starts and threshold rule but not every numerical setting above; the remaining
values are repository conventions.

The fixed two-state JM is refit in January and July on the trailing 3,000
complete feature rows. Ten KMeans++ starts are attempted; coordinate descent
alternates center updates with exact dynamic-programming state updates, and the
lowest objective is retained. The state with higher cumulative training excess
return is favorable. For each online date, the most recent scaler and centers
are frozen, the decoder sees the trailing 3,000 feature rows through that date,
and only its terminal state is emitted.

At the final complete date of each month, each JM lambda and HMM smoothing
candidate is replayed over the preceding eight calendar years. Candidate
signals use the same delay and cost as the outer test. The candidate with the
highest validation Sharpe is used for the following month, subject to the same
delay: a choice made after day \(t\) first affects the position earning the
return at \(t+2\). A candidate needs at least 252 finite validation returns;
ties within \(10^{-12}\) choose the smaller value. The online replay,
eight-year window, monthly update, delayed strategy Sharpe objective, and common
selection procedure for JM and HMM follow Shu et al. Section 3.4.3. Their complete candidate
grids and tie conventions are not published. The proxy grids are
\[
\lambda\in\{0,5,15,35,70,150,300,600,1200\}
\]
and
\[
k\in\{0,2,4,6,8,10,20,40,80,160,320,640,1280,2560\}.
\]
The largest-candidate 5\% diagnostic used in earlier repository audits is not
part of the model and never changes a state, trade, cost, or return.

\subsection{Signal timing, costs, and metrics}

Let \(a_t\in\{0,1\}\) be the equity signal formed after observing day \(t\).
Following Shu et al., it is executed at the end of the next trading day. The
first affected return is therefore \(t+2\). If \(w_u\) is the position earning
return \(u\),
\begin{equation}
r^{p,\mathrm{net}}_u
=w_u r^e_u+(1-w_u)r^c_u
-0.001\,|w_u-w_{u-1}|.
\label{eq:net}
\end{equation}
The first observed position receives no artificial opening cost.

Annualized Sharpe is
\begin{equation}
\operatorname{Sharpe}
=\sqrt{252}\,
\frac{\operatorname{mean}(r^{p,\mathrm{net}}_u-r^c_u)}
     {\operatorname{sd}(r^{p,\mathrm{net}}_u)},
\label{eq:sharpe}
\end{equation}
with sample standard deviation. Maximum drawdown includes initial wealth one.
Paper-convention turnover is
\begin{equation}
\operatorname{Turnover}
=\frac12\,252\,\operatorname{mean}|w_u-w_{u-1}|.
\label{eq:turnover}
\end{equation}
Thus an equity-to-cash-to-equity round trip counts as one unit. The repository's
old display omitted the one-half, but transaction costs always used the full
one-way position change. Correcting the display changed no return, Sharpe, or
drawdown.

For market \(m\) and prespecified variant \(v\), the economic benchmark gap is
\begin{equation}
G_m(v)=\operatorname{Sharpe}_{v,m}
-\max\{\operatorname{Sharpe}_{\mathrm{BH},m},
       \operatorname{Sharpe}_{\mathrm{HMM},m}\}.
\label{eq:gap}
\end{equation}
A local pass requires \(G_m(v)>0\). The cross-market target requires the same
variant to pass all three markets.

\section{Models and hypotheses}

\subsection{Fixed JM}

For \(T\) standardized observations \(x_0,\ldots,x_{T-1}\in\mathbb{R}^3\),
centers \(\theta_0,\theta_1\), and states \(s_t\in\{0,1\}\), fixed JM solves
\begin{equation}
\min_{\Theta,S}
\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\frac12\|x_t-\theta_{s_t}\|_2^2
+\lambda\sum_{t=1}^{T-1}\ind\{s_t\ne s_{t-1}\}.
\label{eq:fixed}
\end{equation}
The first term is k-means-like compactness. The second gives every adjacent
state change the same cost. Larger \(\lambda\) generally creates more
persistence but can delay turning points or collapse a fitted path to one
state.

\subsection{Evidence-adaptive transition costs}

With fixed centers, let
\begin{equation}
L_t(k)=\frac12\|x_t-\theta_k\|_2^2.
\label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
A time-varying decoder minimizes
\begin{equation}
\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}L_t(s_t)
+\sum_{t=1}^{T-1}C_t(s_{t-1},s_t),
\qquad C_t(i,i)=0.
\label{eq:tvobjective}
\end{equation}
For destination state \(j\), the exact recurrence is
\begin{equation}
V_t(j)=L_t(j)+\min_i\{V_{t-1}(i)+C_t(i,j)\}.
\label{eq:dp}
\end{equation}
The complexity remains \(O(TK^2)\), linear in \(T\) for two states.

At each refit, the robust loss scale is the raw median absolute deviation of
all finite state-loss entries on the same trailing 3,000 complete feature rows
used to fit the scaler and centers:
\begin{equation}
q_{\mathrm{train}}
=\operatorname{median}_{u,k}
\left|L_u(k)-\operatorname{median}_{v,\ell}L_v(\ell)\right|>0.
\label{eq:q}
\end{equation}
No epsilon or future-dependent fallback is used. Adaptive experiments retain
the fixed-JM scaler and fitted centers for each refit and lambda; they alter
online decoding, not center fitting.

The arrival-day rule discounts a transition only when the current observation
fits the destination better:
\begin{equation}
C^A_t(i,j)=\lambda_0\exp\!\left[
-\beta\tanh\!\left(
\frac{[L_t(i)-L_t(j)]_+}{q_{\mathrm{train}}}
\right)\right],\qquad i\ne j.
\label{eq:arrival}
\end{equation}
For \(\beta\ge0\),
\(\lambda_0e^{-\beta}\le C^A_t(i,j)\le\lambda_0\). The frozen scenarios are
\(\beta\in\{0,\log2,\log4\}\), permitting maximum discounts of 0\%, 50\%, and
75\%. At \(\beta=0\), every off-diagonal cost is exactly \(\lambda_0\), so the
objective, states, monthly choices, positions, trades, costs, and returns equal
fixed JM.

Arrival evidence is also the current observation loss, so the same gap enters
twice. In the two-state case define
\[
d_t=V_t(1)-V_t(0),\quad g_t=L_t(1)-L_t(0),\quad
a_t=C_t(0,1),\quad b_t=C_t(1,0).
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
d_t=g_t+\operatorname{clip}(d_{t-1},-b_t,a_t).
\label{eq:binary}
\end{equation}
On an evidence-supported active boundary under arrival adaptation,
\begin{equation}
\left|\frac{\partial d_t}{\partial g_t}\right|
=1+\frac{\beta C_t}{q_{\mathrm{train}}}
\sech^2\!\left(\frac{|g_t|}{q_{\mathrm{train}}}\right)\ge1.
\label{eq:amplification}
\end{equation}
The inequality is strict for positive \(\beta\) and \(\lambda_0\), strict
evidence, and an active boundary away from ties. This is a branch identity, not
a general instability theorem.

The lagged rule removes direct same-day reuse:
\begin{equation}
C^L_t(i,j)=\lambda_0\exp\!\left[
-\beta\tanh\!\left(
\frac{[L_{t-1}(i)-L_{t-1}(j)]_+}{q_{\mathrm{train}}}
\right)\right],\qquad i\ne j.
\label{eq:lagged}
\end{equation}
It retains the bounds and beta-zero identity. On January and July refit dates,
the previous row's loss is recomputed with the scaler and centers fitted through
\(t\). The rule is causal at \(t\), although on those dates the evidence term
is not strictly \(\F_{t-1}\)-measurable.

Direction-specific costs require care. For a binary path with constant
\(a=C(0,1)\) and \(b=C(1,0)\),
\begin{equation}
aN_{01}+bN_{10}
=\frac{a+b}{2}N_{\mathrm{switch}}
+\frac{a-b}{2}(s_{T-1}-s_0).
\label{eq:directed}
\end{equation}
Constant asymmetry is therefore a symmetric switching cost plus an endpoint
bias, not independent control of bull and bear durations.

The pair-balanced lagged alternative is
\begin{equation}
C^B_t(i,j)=\lambda_0\left[
1-(1-e^{-\beta})
\tanh\!\left(
\frac{L_{t-1}(i)-L_{t-1}(j)}{q_{\mathrm{train}}}
\right)\right],\qquad i\ne j.
\label{eq:balanced}
\end{equation}
It satisfies
\begin{equation}
C^B_t(i,j)+C^B_t(j,i)=2\lambda_0,
\label{eq:pairsum}
\end{equation}
with bounds
\(\lambda_0e^{-\beta}\le C^B_t(i,j)\le
\lambda_0(2-e^{-\beta})\), and again nests fixed JM at \(\beta=0\).

A separate training-prefix separation gate was considered. With standardized
training rows \(z_u\), fitted centers \(\mu_k\), and \(A_k\) the rows strictly
nearer center \(k\), it used
\[
D=\|\mu_0-\mu_1\|,
\quad \rho_k=\operatorname{median}_{u\in A_k}\|z_u-\mu_k\|,
\quad R=\frac{D}{D+\rho_0+\rho_1}.
\]
The statistic is defined and lies in \([0,1]\) only when both \(A_k\) are
nonempty and the denominator is positive. The completed performance-free
diagnostic was inconclusive, so the gate was not promoted to a P\&L study.

\subsection{Five simple challengers}

The adaptive models change transition geometry. Five simpler challengers test
whether a smaller modification is more useful. All retain the same complete-row
mask, fit window, refit schedule, validation period where applicable, delay,
cost, and evaluation dates.

\paragraph{Static lambda.}
The three-feature fixed JM uses \(\lambda=50\) throughout, removing monthly
lambda selection. Shu et al. use 50 as an illustrative value in their fitted
parameter and COVID-19 figures; it is not their undisclosed final strategy
lambda.

\paragraph{Downside deviation only.}
With training-standardized downside deviation \(z^{DD}_t\),
\begin{equation}
\min_{\theta,S}
\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\frac12(z^{DD}_t-\theta_{s_t})^2
+\lambda\sum_{t=1}^{T-1}\ind\{s_t\ne s_{t-1}\}.
\label{eq:ddonly}
\end{equation}
The unchanged raw lambda grid makes this a deliberately simple but confounded
test: one standardized loss coordinate is compared with lambdas previously
applied to three coordinates.

\paragraph{Two-observation confirmation.}
For fixed-JM state \(s_t\) and confirmed state \(a_t\),
\begin{equation}
a_t=
\begin{cases}
s_t,&s_t=s_{t-1},\\
a_{t-1},&s_t\ne s_{t-1}.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:confirmation}
\end{equation}
This removes a one-observation excursion and delays a persistent change by one
observation.

\paragraph{Return-aware fitting.}
At training cutoff \(T-1\), let \(y_{u+2}\) be excess return two trading rows
after feature row \(u\). Its mean and population standard deviation are
estimated only from matured targets already observed by the cutoff, producing
\(\widetilde y_{u+2}\). Let \(m_u=1\) only for those matured targets. The
objective is
\begin{equation}
\min_{\Theta,\mu,S}
\sum_{u=0}^{T-1}\frac12\|x_u-\theta_{s_u}\|_2^2
+\frac{\gamma}{2}\sum_{u=0}^{T-1}m_u
(\widetilde y_{u+2}-\mu_{s_u})^2
+\lambda\sum_{u=1}^{T-1}\ind\{s_u\ne s_{u-1}\}.
\label{eq:returnaware}
\end{equation}
The tested value is \(\gamma=1\); \(\gamma=0\) is an exact routed fixed-JM
control. Online decoding uses current features and frozen feature centers only,
so no future return enters an online decision.

\paragraph{Robust feature loss.}
The last challenger replaces half-squared Euclidean loss with cityblock loss,
\begin{equation}
\min_{\Theta,S}
\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\|x_t-\theta_{s_t}\|_1
+\lambda\sum_{t=1}^{T-1}\ind\{s_t\ne s_{t-1}\},
\label{eq:l1}
\end{equation}
whose fixed-state center is the componentwise median. L1 and half-squared L2
have different units, so reuse of the raw grid is an uncalibrated exploratory
comparison.

These loss-gap rules are candidate extensions rather than a global priority
claim. Adjacent work includes continuous JMs \citep{aydinhan2024}, sparse
feature selection \citep{nystrup2021,cortese2023}, and generalized information
criteria for sparse JMs \citep{cortese2024}.

\subsection{Three-times DD loss control}

DD-only removes two coordinates and also reduces observation-loss scale
relative to every unchanged lambda. The prespecified coordinate-count control
keeps the same DD feature, calendar, training-only standardization, raw lambda
grid, refits, monthly selection, timing, and cost, but solves
\begin{equation}
J_3(\theta,S;\lambda)
=\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\frac32(z^{DD}_t-\theta_{s_t})^2
+\lambda\sum_{t=1}^{T-1}\ind\{s_t\ne s_{t-1}\}.
\label{eq:scaleddd}
\end{equation}
If \(J_1\) denotes the ordinary DD-only objective in
Equation~\eqref{eq:ddonly}, then
\begin{equation}
J_3(\theta,S;\lambda)=3J_1(\theta,S;\lambda/3).
\label{eq:scaledidentity}
\end{equation}
For the same data, starts, and tie rules, scaled DD at lambda \(\lambda\)
therefore has the same path ranking as ordinary DD at \(\lambda/3\). The
absolute optimizer tolerance is scaled mechanically from \(10^{-8}\) to
\(3\times10^{-8}\), preserving its normalized meaning under
Equation~\eqref{eq:scaledidentity}. The factor three matches coordinate count;
it is a theory-fixed diagnostic, not an empirical calibration or proof that the
three fitted residual coordinates contribute equal loss. The raw grid is not
expanded, so most \(\lambda/3\) counterparts are absent from the monthly search.

\subsection{Extending the sample to 2026 and a selection-clean window}

Every walk-forward result in this paper is already out-of-sample in the causal
sense: at each date the model fits preprocessing, centers, and the penalty on
trailing data only, so no future observation enters any single decision. What
the through-2023 span is \emph{not} free of is selection bias. DD-only was
promoted after inspecting that span across many exploratory studies, so its
through-2023 performance is optimistic for the same reason any model chosen on
a sample looks good on it. To separate these two axes we extended the public
proxies to 30 June 2026 and, once, opened the 2024-01-02 to 2026-06-30 window
for the pre-declared DD-only variant. That window is the only span the choice
of DD-only could not have used. The extension is opened after a precondition: a
fresh replication of the sealed 2023 sample with the current source reproduced
the accepted baseline on all 123 scientific output files byte-for-byte, and the
extended series overlap the sealed sample with maximum absolute difference zero
on every shared row.

\subsection{Interpretation and limitations}

No single tested variant passes all three markets. The local winners are
DD-only in the US and lagged evidence in Germany; no JM beats \BH{} in Japan.
Choosing those models separately after seeing each market would be an ex-post
upper envelope, not one deployable rule.

The adaptive experiments show why candidate-path behavior and selected-strategy
behavior can differ. A mechanism can reduce reversals within candidate paths
yet cause monthly validation to choose different lambdas, increasing activity
after selection. The large path/choice interaction and the Japanese results
rule out a simple universal claim that evidence discounting makes the model
better.

The DD loss-scale result resolves one ambiguity but leaves two qualifications.
Most of DD-only's Sharpe gain over fixed JM survives the three-times loss, so
raw scale is not the main empirical explanation. Yet the fixed candidate grid
binds in Germany and Japan, and monthly selection still chooses collapsed
one-state fits there. The result therefore supports continued study of feature
content; it does not establish that DD is a sufficient feature or that the two
Sortino coordinates are universally harmful.

The main limitations are substantive. Proxy instruments and test periods are
not definition-equivalent to the parent study. Final parent grids are unknown.
Only three correlated markets are used. Adaptive centers are not jointly refit.
Beta is scenario-fixed rather than estimated. DD-only and L1 alter loss units.
One-state fitted routes are frequent. The data through 2023 have been inspected
repeatedly, and no confidence interval, multiple-testing correction, or
untouched confirmation sample supports a performance claim. Even a strategy
that beats both benchmarks would demonstrate an economically useful signal,
not the existence of a unique true latent market regime.

\section{Conclusion}

Shu et al. begin with an investor rather than an algorithm. A market/cash
strategy makes the economic value of a regime signal visible; HMM supplies a
benchmark; fixed JM asks whether charging for every state change creates a more
persistent and useful path. Their licensed 1990--2023 evidence says yes in the
US, Germany, and Japan.

On the later public proxy sample, fixed JM does not recover that ordering. The
mathematical extensions remain valid: time-varying penalties preserve exact
dynamic programming, beta zero is fixed JM, the arrival rule has an explicit
same-day amplification branch, and the pair-balanced rule has an exact
two-direction identity. Their economic effects are heterogeneous. The frozen
arrival trade-off rule is not supported, lagging produces a large local German
improvement but increases Japanese activity, and pair balancing does not
improve on lagging overall.

The simpler suite produces the strongest clue. Downside deviation alone raises
fixed-JM Sharpe in every market and beats both same-sample controls in the US.
The three-times loss control preserves most of that advantage over fixed JM,
which makes removal of the two Sortino coordinates the stronger explanation
than raw loss scale alone. It still wins only in the US, however, while the
finite grid binds and one-state routes remain selected in Germany and Japan.
No single tested JM beats both \BH{} and HMM in every market. The contribution
is therefore a verified set of candidate objectives and a sharper empirical
failure map, not a performance claim. Such a claim would require a
prespecified model evaluated on data that have not already shaped it. We took a
first step: after extending the proxies to 2026, we opened the selection-clean
2024-2026 window once for DD-only. On the full walk-forward through 2026 its US
edge is unchanged, but the isolated post-selection window is a short bull that
neither confirms nor refutes that edge. A conclusive test needs a longer
selection-independent sample or a drawdown-conditional readout, and remains
future work.
