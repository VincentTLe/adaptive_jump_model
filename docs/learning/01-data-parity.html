Question 1

Is this really the same market data?

Two files can both say “S&P 500” and still describe different investments.

Remember this: before comparing models, make sure they receive the same economic input.

A $100 example

You buy a stock for $100. One year later its quoted price is still $100, but it paid a $4 dividend.

Starting wealth$100
Price-only ending wealth$100
Total-return ending wealth$104

The price series reports 0% growth. The total-return series, which reinvests dividends, reports 4%. Neither calculation is broken; they answer different questions.

One market name can hide several choices

Equity series

Price index: price changes only.

Total-return index: price changes plus reinvested distributions.

Cash series

A “three-month rate” may be a Treasury bill, an interbank rate, or another proxy. Quotation and timing also differ.

Local currency means measuring each market in its home currency: US dollars, euros or Deutsche marks as applicable, and Japanese yen. This avoids silently adding exchange-rate effects.

Paper data and current project data

The paper reports Bloomberg daily total-return indices and local three-month Treasury-bill data from Global Financial Data. Those exact paid series are not in this repository.

The current project uses documented free substitutes. A proxy is a substitute chosen to represent the unavailable original. It can test whether the broad result appears again, but it cannot establish exact replication.

Important: a different result may come from the proxy data, an implementation difference, random numerical behavior, or a real lack of robustness. The result alone cannot tell us which explanation is correct.

Three honest labels

Exact replication

Same data definitions and protocol; matching numerical results within a stated tolerance.

Proxy replication

Documented substitute data; useful evidence, but not a numerical reproduction.

Non-replication means the required comparison did not reappear. It does not, by itself, prove that the paper is wrong.

Optional deeper look: why history length matters

The model repeatedly estimates itself from the previous 3,000 trading days and selects settings from eight years of past validation. If a proxy begins later, the first valid test date must also move later. The available history therefore changes the experiment, not just the number of rows.

Check your understanding

Question: If a free Nikkei file contains only prices while the paper used total returns, is that exact replication?

Answer: No. It is a price-index proxy and must be labeled as such.
