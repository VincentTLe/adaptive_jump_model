Part II · Honest Research

Backtesting Without Seeing The Future

At the moment a strategy acts, which observations and decisions could really have existed?

Beginner levelCausal timing laboratoryPaper Sections 3.1 and 4.1
The opening question

Can Monday's signal protect you from a return already earned?

You hold $100 in the market. A model uses Monday's closing value and produces a cash signal after Monday closes. Tuesday's market return is -10%. A cheating backtest applies the cash position to Tuesday as if the order existed before Monday's close. It reports about $99.90 after a 10-basis-point switch cost. But the signal was not available soon enough for that position.

Under the paper's one-day trading delay, the Monday end-of-day signal can be executed only at the end of Tuesday and applies starting Wednesday. Your portfolio remains in the market for Tuesday, so $100 becomes $90. If Wednesday is the first cash day and the one-way switch costs 0.10%, the switch removes nine cents from the $90 portfolio, leaving $89.91 before any cash return. Honest timing produces a very different path.

Impossible same-close backtest

About $99.90

The Monday-close signal is applied before Tuesday's loss even though it did not exist when that position had to be chosen.

Causal one-day delay

$100 → $90 → $89.91

Tuesday remains market-exposed. Cash starts Wednesday, and the switch cost is charged when the position changes.

This is not a minor implementation detail. A strategy is a timeline of information and action. If any future price, future state, future scaler, future model choice, or unavailable close influences an earlier position, the measured return is not an out-of-sample result that could have occurred.

The idea to rememberFor every return, write four timestamps: when the observation became available, when the decision was made, when the position could be executed, and which later return interval that position earned. If the order is impossible, the backtest is invalid.
Words before notation

Name each boundary before calculating performance

Observation time

When an input value can be known. A date printed on a series is not enough if the value is published later.

Decision time

When the model and frozen rule turn available inputs into a signal or hyperparameter choice.

Execution time

When the requested position can actually be established after calculation and operational delay.

Earned interval

The return period controlled by the executed position. It must begin after execution, never before it.

Leakage

Any use of information from after a decision to improve that decision, directly or through preprocessing, labels, selection, or evaluation.

Walk-forward

A procedure that repeatedly trains on the past, makes the next permitted decision, advances time, and never revises old outputs using future observations.

A training window supplies observations used to estimate model parameters. A validation window uses later but still historical observations to choose among candidate hyperparameters. An outer test evaluates the selected procedure on observations that did not select it. A locked confirmation period remains untouched until the complete hypothesis and pipeline are frozen.

Rolling means a fixed-length window moves forward, dropping old observations as new ones arrive. Expanding means the start stays fixed while the window grows. Neither is automatically better; the paper and implementation must agree. A refit cadence states how often parameters are estimated again. Online inference can occur daily even if a model is refitted less frequently.

Scaling transforms features to a common center and spread. If a scaler uses the whole dataset, future crashes change the mean and standard deviation applied to past observations. That is leakage even if the model itself receives rows in date order. Train-only preprocessing means each decision uses a scaler fitted only on its permitted historical window.

Prefix invariance is a causal test: append future rows and verify that all previously emitted states, choices, and positions remain unchanged. Common sample means competing models are compared on identical eligible dates. A model should not receive a better score merely because its missing rows remove difficult periods.

One-way turnover is the absolute change in portfolio position. Moving from fully invested, position 1, to cash, position 0, creates turnover 1. A cost quoted in basis points uses 10,000 basis points per unit return: 10 bps equals 0.001 or 0.10%. A later return cannot repay a cost that the backtest forgot to charge.

Selection fairness compares complete pipelines rather than isolated fitted models. Two methods can share outer dates while one searches many more variants, receives more restarts, or was repeatedly redesigned after seeing outcomes. Nested time-series selection and an untouched outer comparison help account for that advantage. A grid-boundary check asks whether the largest candidate is selected too often. If it is, the search range may be inadequate; the frozen project stops before opening OOS metrics rather than expanding the grid after seeing performance.

Now add model selection. At a month-end decision, each candidate smoothing value is evaluated over the trailing validation window using only online states that would have existed then. Candidate strategy returns use the same delay, cash convention, and transaction cost as the later test. The best eligible candidate is chosen by the frozen tie rule and used for the following month after its own delay.

A common shortcut computes all candidate states across the full sample, chooses the candidate with the best final Sharpe ratio, and then draws an apparently historical strategy. Every early position now depends on years of future returns. Calling the rows "out of sample" does not fix selection performed on the same outcomes.

A common failure: fitting one scaler before splitting time

Suppose ten calm historical observations have values near zero and a future crash has value -20. A full-sample mean and standard deviation include that crash. Past calm values are transformed relative to information that had not happened, possibly changing cluster distances and states. The correct process fits the scaler inside each trailing training window and transforms that same window for the current decision.

Another failure maps anonymous state labels using test returns. State 0 and state 1 have no built-in economic meaning. The risky-versus-cash mapping must be learned from training information and then frozen for the associated outer period. Choosing the label that produced the better test return is another form of outcome leakage.

Connection to the named paper

The paper explicitly delays the signal

Section 3.1 of Shu, Yu, and Mulvey (2024), arXiv:2402.05272v3 says an ideal end-of-day-t signal might be applied on t+1, but assumes a one-day operational delay. A changed allocation is executed at the end of t+1 and applies starting t+2.

Section 3.4.3 selects JM jump penalty or HMM smoothing monthly using strategy Sharpe over an eight-year online validation lookback. The validation strategy includes the same one-day delay and a 10-basis-point one-way transaction cost. The paper says the selected hyperparameter is itself applied with delay. Twelve years of training history plus eight years of validation history make 1990 the stated out-of-sample start when data begin in 1970.

Section 4 evaluates each market independently from 1990 through 2023. Section 4.1 and Table 5 test delays of 1, 5, and 10 days, corresponding to signals applied to returns at t+2, t+6, and t+11. The same delay is used in validation and testing so smoothing selection reflects the operational scenario.

Connection to this project

The causal rule is explicit and tested

The frozen timing and cost settings live in research.toml: primary delay 1, signal-to-return offset 2, robustness delays 1/5/10, one-way cost 10 bps, and no cost for the initial allocation. The config validator rejects a primary offset that is not delay plus one.

src/adaptive_jump/backtest.py implements the accounting only after the theory. The essential lines are short:

position = signal.shift(delay_trading_days + 1)
gross = position * equity_return + (1 - position) * cash_return
turnover = abs(position - previous_position)
strategy_return = gross - turnover * cost_bps / 10_000

src/adaptive_jump/walkforward.py creates candidate strategy returns with that same delay and cost before monthly selection. It scores only dates inside the trailing eight-calendar-year validation window through the decision date. Tests append altered future data and require past features, choices, and backtest rows to remain unchanged.

When metrics are opened, all three models for one market and delay are intersected onto identical complete dates. The verifier later rejects trade paths whose delayed positions do not equal the earlier signals, whose turnover differs from position changes, or whose gross and net returns fail the accounting identity. These are implementation checks, not evidence that the chosen model is economically correct.

The first eligible proxy OOS date is also computed rather than assumed. It must occur after the 3,000th complete feature observation plus eight calendar years of validation history and cannot precede the requested start. This shifts the paper's nominal 1990 boundary when free-source coverage is shorter, preserving chronology while requiring the proxy-replication label from Chapter 5.

The first equation is the chronology rule. A delay of zero still shifts by one row because an end-of-day signal cannot earn the return that just ended. A delay of one shifts by two rows. The second equation chooses market or cash return using the position. The third subtracts cost only when the binary position changes.

For the opening example, qMonday=0 requests cash and d=1. Therefore cash position 0 first controls Wednesday's return. Tuesday position remains 1 and earns -10%, taking wealth to $90. Wednesday turnover is |0−1|=1; 10 bps equals 0.001, so the cost is $90 × 0.001 = $0.09.

Fair model comparison adds one more operation. For model date sets S1,S2,S3, evaluate all models on S=S1∩S2∩S3. The intersection removes model-specific date advantages. It may shorten the sample, so the resulting start and observation count must be reported.

Evidence and limitations

Causal code is necessary, not sufficient

What tests establish

Frozen offset, cost arithmetic, future-prefix invariance, trailing selection, binary positions, common dates, and recomputable trade identities behave as implemented.

What tests do not establish

That delay and cost assumptions match every investor, that proxies match the paper, or that a regime signal has stable future economic value.

Empirical boundaryThe v7 evidence through 2023 was produced under the frozen causal pipeline and should be read from its verified generated report. No metric is copied or recalculated in this lesson.
Confirmation boundaryA pointwise causal OOS path can still be overused. Repeatedly viewing it while changing features, grids, or models turns it into development evidence. A new confirmatory claim requires a genuinely untouched period or another prospective design.

Real trading adds bid-ask spreads, market impact, taxes, order size, execution uncertainty, and operational constraints beyond a fixed 10-basis-point scenario. The paper's cost and delay assumptions make the comparison more realistic than a frictionless same-close backtest, but they do not make it production-ready.

Retrieval practice

Audit the clock before reading the Sharpe ratio

Recall

An end-of-day-t signal has a one-day trading delay. Which return can the new position first earn?

Choose an answer, then check the explanation.
Calculation: one-way switch cost

A $250 portfolio moves from position 1 to 0 with a 10-bps one-way cost. Turnover is 1 and the cost rate is 10 / 10,000 = 0.001, so the cost is $0.25. A later move back to 1 incurs another one-way cost.

Error diagnosis: full-sample standardization

Future observations affect the center and scale assigned to past features. Fit the scaler only on each permitted trailing training window, then transform observations used for that decision.

Advisor explanation

"For a signal at end of t, our one-day delay shifts the position to t+2. Validation uses the same delay and costs as testing, preprocessing is fitted on trailing history only, future rows cannot alter past decisions, and models are scored on common dates."

How to explain this to your advisor

Short version: "I audit information time before performance. Every feature, model fit, candidate choice, state label, position, cost, and return must be available in causal order."

Important qualification: "Passing leakage and accounting tests validates the evaluation machinery. It does not prove the model is useful, the proxy data match the paper, or the inspected sample remains confirmatory."
