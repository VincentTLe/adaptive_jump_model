Market Regimes And Persistence

Is the model predicting tomorrow, or recognizing that today's market environment has changed?

Beginner levelCompletes Part IInteractive latency timeline
The opening question

When does one bad day become a bad period?

You hold $100 in the market. On Monday it loses 2%. That single observation may be ordinary noise. If losses, large price movements, and weak risk-adjusted returns continue for several weeks, the pattern begins to look like a different market environment. A regime model tries to distinguish temporary variation from a persistent change.

Weather provides a useful first analogy. One cold afternoon does not necessarily mean winter has arrived. A sustained combination of lower temperature, shorter daylight, and repeated cold days supports the winter label. The label summarizes a collection of observations; it does not determine the exact temperature tomorrow. Market regimes play a similar role, though the data and consequences are financial rather than meteorological.

One isolated loss
$100 → $98

A single negative return is evidence, but not enough by itself to establish a persistent unfavorable regime.

Persistent adverse pattern
Repeated losses + elevated risk

Waiting for evidence reduces false alarms, but waiting too long exposes wealth to more of the downturn.

One idea to rememberThe paper identifies the regime that appears to prevail now and relies on persistence. It does not claim to predict tomorrow's exact price or the exact day a future crash will begin.
Build the vocabulary

State, regime, switch, and persistence

A state is a model's compact label for a pattern. The paper uses two states. State 0 is labeled favorable and state 1 unfavorable after fitting. The raw numbers carry no natural meaning: another implementation could swap them and describe the same partition. Economic labeling must therefore be explicit and reproducible.

A regime is an extended period during which market observations share relatively homogeneous behavior. “Homogeneous” does not mean identical. Returns inside a favorable regime can still be negative, and returns inside an unfavorable regime can still be sharply positive. The label describes a distributional pattern over time, not a rule that every day must have one sign.

A regime switch is a change from one state to another. A turning point is the boundary between regimes, usually easier to describe after more data have arrived. An online investor cannot use a turning point dated with future hindsight. The model must decide using only observations available by the current date.

Persistence means that a state tends to continue rather than flip immediately. Persistence is economically important because a signal observed tonight can affect a position only later. If states change every day, a delayed strategy may trade yesterday's environment after it has already disappeared.

A hidden state is not directly observed like a closing price. Researchers infer it from returns or features. There is no daily official answer sheet saying “the true state today is 1.” Business-cycle dates and named bear markets may offer retrospective context, but they arrive late, use different definitions, or depend on future observations.

A regime can be market-specific. The United States, Germany, and Japan do not have to enter an unfavorable state on the same date. The paper fits each equity index independently in local currency; it does not infer one global state and apply it everywhere. This matters when interpreting international results: agreement across three separately modeled markets is stronger pattern evidence than three assets mechanically driven by one shared label, but different histories also make exact comparisons harder.

Observation

A measured return or feature available at a date.

Hidden state

A model label inferred from a sequence of observations.

Position

The market-or-cash allocation that eventually earns a return.

A critical distinction

Identification is not next-day prediction

A prediction exercise might ask: using information available tonight, what will tomorrow's return be? The paper asks a different question: using observations through tonight, which regime best describes the current market? The inferred state becomes useful only if the current pattern persists long enough for the delayed position to benefit.

Suppose five difficult days have already occurred. A model identifies an unfavorable state at the end of the fifth day. It has not predicted the first five losses; they helped produce the label. If weakness continues, moving to cash may avoid later losses. If the market rebounds immediately, the signal may be late and miss gains. The financial value comes from timely recognition plus persistence, not clairvoyance.

This difference changes how evidence should be described. Words such as “forecasted crash” can exaggerate what an unsupervised state model does. Better language is “online inferred unfavorable regime.” Chapter 9 will show that an HMM has transition probabilities and could produce a formal next-state probability, but the paper's trading signal uses the terminal state decoded for the current window.

Common failureShading a historical crash with a state learned using data from after the crash is in-sample description, not real-time identification. Online evaluation must rebuild the state available at each past date without future observations.
Learning without an answer key

Why regime identification is unsupervised

In supervised learning, training examples contain both inputs and known target labels. A spam classifier might see messages already marked spam or not spam. In unsupervised learning, the algorithm searches for structure without an observed target label for every row. HMMs and Jump Models infer state assignments because daily financial regimes are unobserved.

Unsupervised does not mean arbitrary. The model has an objective, assumptions, number of states, features, and initialization. The fitted states can be characterized by their return and risk behavior. In the paper, HMM states are ordered by conditional volatility. Jump Model states are labeled using cumulative excess return over the training window. These conventions turn anonymous cluster numbers into favorable and unfavorable economic labels.

Because no perfect ground-truth sequence exists, the downstream 0/1 strategy becomes part of evaluation. The question is not only whether clusters look intuitive on a chart, but whether online states support downside reduction after costs and delay. This practical evaluation still does not prove that a state is the unique “true” regime; another useful partition may exist.

Unsupervised state numbers can also suffer label switching. A fitted solution with raw state 0 representing turbulence can be mathematically equivalent to another fit that calls the same cluster raw state 1. Comparing raw numbers across refits would then create false switches. The implementation must apply a canonical economic ordering—such as low versus high volatility or higher versus lower cumulative excess return—before states become signals. Tests should verify behavior under a state permutation rather than assume an optimizer always returns labels in one order.

Worked example one

Fast signals and stable signals trade advantages

A model that changes state after every surprising return reacts quickly. It can also mistake noise for a durable shift, create high turnover, and reverse before a trade is executed. A model that strongly favors persistence produces fewer switches and may be easier to trade, but it can recognize genuine turning points late.

Low persistence

Quick response, many state flips, more false alarms, more trading, and greater sensitivity to daily noise.

High persistence

Stable regimes and lower turnover, but potentially greater latency at the beginning and end of a downturn.

The HMM and JM encourage persistence differently. An HMM learns transition probabilities, then the paper smooths terminal HMM states with a trailing median-style filter. A Jump Model directly charges a fixed objective penalty whenever adjacent states differ. The two mechanisms will receive separate mathematical chapters; here the important point is that smoothing cannot be judged independently from execution timing.

A common failure: choose maximum smoothness

A state sequence that never changes has perfect persistence and zero switching turnover, but it cannot respond to changing conditions. Smoothness is not the objective by itself. The paper selects the smoothing setting by past validation strategy Sharpe under the same delay and cost assumptions used for the corresponding test.

Two clocks

Model latency and trading delay are different

Identification latency is the time needed for observations to convince the model that a state changed. It is a property of data, features, model, and smoothing. Trading delay is the operational wait after a signal exists before the new position earns a return. It may reflect calculation time, approval, execution constraints, or the protocol's realism rule.

If an unfavorable period begins on day 1 and the model needs three daily observations, its bad-state signal first exists at the end of day 3. Under the paper's one-day trading delay, an end-of-day-3 signal controls return on day 5. Days 1 through 4 remain market-exposed. The model may have correctly recognized a persistent event yet still miss its beginning.

The lab deliberately visualizes only entry into the unfavorable interval. A real strategy must also recognize the end. Excessive persistence can leave the portfolio in cash during a rebound. The full online path therefore contains entry latency, exit latency, delay, missed losses, missed gains, and transaction costs.

Exit latency can be especially costly after a sharp crash because rebounds may be concentrated in a small number of large positive days. A model that successfully avoided part of the decline can still finish behind buy-and-hold if it re-enters too late. This is why inspecting only the red bear shading during the falling portion of a chart is incomplete. Cumulative wealth after the shaded interval and all unshaded false alarms must remain in the evaluation.

Layered mathematics

Represent states and switches

Let each state be either favorable 0 or unfavorable 1. A switch occurs when today's state differs from yesterday's state. The indicator contributes one for a switch and zero for no switch. Summing the indicators counts the changes in a complete state path.

st∈{0,1} switches= ∑t=1T−1 1{st−1≠st}

st is the inferred state at date index t. T is the number of state observations. This switch count later appears inside the Jump Model objective multiplied by the jump penalty λ.

The economic signal is the opposite binary value under the frozen labels:

signalt=1−st

If state is favorable 0, the requested risky signal is 1. If state is unfavorable 1, the requested risky signal is 0. The held position is a delayed version of that signal, not necessarily the same-day value.

Paper connection

The paper explicitly avoids “regime prediction”

Section 3.2 of Shu, Yu, and Mulvey (2024), arXiv:2402.05272v3 describes HMMs and JMs as unsupervised regime-identification models. It states that the models interpret the prevailing state from features available at the end of day t, rather than predicting day t+1 returns. Profitability relies on the identified regime persisting.

Section 3.4.2 explains online JM inference and distinguishes identification latency from trading delay. Table 3 compares average state shifts under different HMM filters and JM penalties. Figure 4 contrasts a hindsight in-sample regime with the online regime around the 2020 crash, showing delayed recognition. Section 4.1 tests trading delays of 1, 5, and 10 days.

Paper argumentJM persistence is proposed as economically useful because delayed signals must survive long enough to reduce downside risk without excessive turnover. The paper supports this with empirical comparisons; persistence alone is not sufficient proof of better strategy performance.

Evidence and limits

A plausible regime story can still fail out of sample

Established here

Persistent states make delayed allocation conceptually possible, and latency consumes part of an adverse interval before protection begins.

Not established

The toy known regime is not evidence that real hidden regimes exist uniquely or that either model identifies them profitably.

Scientific boundaryThe timeline supplies known regime boundaries for teaching. Real online research never receives those boundaries as contemporaneous labels.

Regime labels can also become narratives after the fact. A red shaded area near a famous crash looks persuasive, but visual alignment does not quantify missed rebounds, false alarms, costs, parameter selection, or other markets. The paper's full table and the project's frozen gate are stronger evidence structures than a selected chart.

Finally, two states are a modeling choice rather than a law of markets. A real market may contain calm growth, volatile growth, slow decline, crash, recovery, and other patterns. Two states compress that variety into an interpretable favorable/unfavorable decision suited to the binary strategy. Adding more states could fit richer behavior but would increase estimation, labeling, and selection complexity. The course therefore explains the frozen two-state experiment before discussing any extension.

Retrieval practice

Separate recognition, persistence, and execution

Recall

What does the paper's daily terminal state primarily claim to do?

Calculation: latency plus delay

An adverse regime lasts 10 days. Identification takes 4 days and trading delay is 1 day. The first five adverse days remain unprotected, leaving five protected adverse days in this entry-only teaching calculation.

Error diagnosis: zero state switches

A sequence that never switches is maximally persistent but cannot adapt. Persistence must balance false alarms against turning-point latency and must be selected without looking at test outcomes.

Cumulative recall from Chapters 1–3

The inferred state maps to a delayed market-or-cash position. That position earns simple total or cash return. Compounded returns create wealth, and risk metrics evaluate the path. None of those later objects can be credited to the model before the signal was available.

Advisor explanation

“The paper performs online regime identification, not exact-return forecasting. It hopes the state identified today persists long enough for a t+2 position to benefit. The Jump Model's persistence is therefore economically relevant, but it must still win an out-of-sample comparison.”

How to explain this to your advisor

Short version: “A regime is a persistent pattern inferred from data, not an observed answer or a promise about tomorrow. The strategy acts after identification and execution delays.”

Important qualification: “More persistence reduces noise and turnover but increases latency. The best smoothing level must be selected on past validation, not chosen after seeing the test.”
