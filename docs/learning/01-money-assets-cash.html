Part I · Money, Returns, And Risk

Money, Assets, And Cash

What does it actually mean when a model says, “leave the market and move to cash”?

Beginner levelNo mathematics requiredOne interactive allocation lab
The opening question

You have $100 tonight. Where can it go?

Imagine that you have exactly $100 and must choose where to keep it for one day. You could buy part of a broad stock-market investment, keep part in a short-term cash-like investment, or divide the money between the two. This simple choice is the economic heart of the entire project. The Hidden Markov Model and Jump Model will eventually produce complicated-looking state sequences, but those states matter only because they control this ordinary allocation decision.

Suppose you put all $100 in the market. If the market gains 5%, your wealth becomes $105. If it loses 5%, your wealth becomes $95. Now suppose a cash investment earns 0.02% for the day. Your $100 becomes $100.02. The cash gain is tiny, but its value is much more stable. The market offers more opportunity and more uncertainty; cash offers less opportunity and less uncertainty.

Market rises 5%
$100 → $105

You receive the full market gain because every dollar was exposed to the market.

Market falls 5%
$100 → $95

The same exposure that created upside also creates loss. “Risky” does not mean “bad”; it means the outcome is uncertain.

One idea to rememberA regime model does not create money by itself. It only supplies a signal that decides whether the strategy holds the risky market asset or the safer cash asset.
Build the vocabulary

Six words that must be clear

Money is the unit in which wealth is measured. In this course we use dollars in toy examples because “$100” is easy to picture. The paper does not convert every market to dollars. It evaluates the United States in US dollars, Germany in its local currency, and Japan in Japanese yen. That choice avoids mixing market performance with exchange-rate movements.

An asset is something that can hold or change economic value. A stock, a government bill, a bank deposit, a bond, a house, and a commodity can all be assets. The project studies only a deliberately narrow choice: one broad equity-market asset and one local cash-like asset for each country.

Equity means ownership. A share of stock represents a small ownership claim on a company. An equity index combines many stocks into one numerical measure. The S&P 500, DAX, and Nikkei 225 are indices. You cannot place a paper index certificate in a wallet, but an index return describes the performance of a portfolio designed to follow those constituent stocks.

An index and an investable fund are related but not identical. The index is a rule and a calculated series: it specifies which securities are represented and how their values are combined. An index fund or exchange-traded fund is a real vehicle that attempts to track that rule. A fund may have management fees, taxes, trading frictions, tracking error, and operational constraints that are absent from an ideal index series. The paper studies index returns because the goal is to compare regime signals on a clean market benchmark. That does not mean a real investor would receive every reported index return exactly.

A risky asset is an asset whose future value is uncertain. “Risky” does not assert that the value will fall. It says that multiple outcomes are possible and that losses can be meaningful. In the paper, the local equity index is the risky asset.

A risk-free asset is a theoretical or practical benchmark whose short-horizon return is treated as known with very low default risk. No real investment is free from every possible risk. Researchers use short-term government bills as an approximation because their repayment horizon is short and sovereign default risk is usually treated as negligible for the experiment. In the current project, some free data sources are weaker proxies, which Chapter 5 will examine carefully.

Cash is used in this course as convenient shorthand for the risk-free side of the strategy. It does not mean paper notes sitting idle under a mattress. The paper's cash side earns the local three-month Treasury-bill return. Therefore, moving to cash does not mean earning exactly zero.

Wealth is the current dollar value of what you own; a return is the change in wealth relative to a starting value. If wealth moves from $100 to $105, the dollar gain is $5 and the return is 5%. Keeping these concepts separate matters because model evaluation combines returns across thousands of days, while a human usually imagines a dollar balance. The same 5% return means a $5 gain on $100 but a $500 gain on $10,000. Returns make different starting wealth levels comparable; wealth shows the economic path experienced by the investor.

Market

Higher uncertain return. Position value is 1.

Cash

Lower, steadier return. Position value is 0.

Signal

A model instruction that eventually determines which position is held.

Worked example

Divide $100 between market and cash

Before studying the paper's all-or-nothing strategy, consider an ordinary mixed portfolio. Put $60 in the market and $40 in cash. Suppose the market gains 10% and cash gains 2% over the chosen period. The market part grows from $60 to $66. The cash part grows from $40 to $40.80. Add the two ending values and the portfolio finishes at $106.80.

PartStarting amountReturnEnding amount
Market$60.0010%$66.00
Cash$40.002%$40.80
Total$100.006.8%$106.80

The portfolio return is not the simple average of 10% and 2%. A simple average would give 6%, but that would pretend that the two assets received equal amounts. The correct result is a weighted return: 60% of the portfolio receives the market return and 40% receives the cash return. That produces 6.8%.

A common failure: confusing amount with return

Suppose someone says, “Cash earned 2, so the portfolio ends at $108.” This confuses a 2% return with two dollars applied to the whole portfolio. Only $40 was in cash, so 2% applies to $40 and contributes $0.80. Units protect us from this mistake: dollars multiply percentages to produce dollar gains. A percentage by itself is not a dollar amount.

From a mixed portfolio to the paper

Why does the paper use only 0 or 1?

The Shu, Yu, and Mulvey paper studies a 0/1 strategy. The number represents the fraction invested in the risky index. A position of 1 means 100% market and 0% cash. A position of 0 means 0% market and 100% cash. Values such as 0.60 are excluded from the strategy. This restriction is intentional, not a claim that real investors must always act in all-or-nothing fashion.

A binary strategy isolates the economic value of regime identification. If the portfolio also optimized many partial weights, several assets, leverage, and diversification, it would become difficult to tell whether performance came from the regime model or from the portfolio optimizer. By switching between only two positions, the experiment asks a cleaner question: did the model identify unfavorable periods well enough that leaving the market helped after delay and transaction costs?

Exposure describes how much of the portfolio is affected by an asset's return. Position 1 gives full market exposure. Position 0 removes market exposure but does not remove every economic risk: the cash instrument still has a return definition, and inflation can reduce purchasing power. Leverage in the paper's metric table is the average risky weight through time. If a strategy holds the market on three quarters of its measured days and cash on one quarter, its average risky exposure is approximately 75%. This describes behavior; by itself it does not tell us whether the timing was skillful.

Leaving the market also creates an opportunity cost. If the model moves to cash before a crash, it may avoid a large loss. If it remains in cash during a rebound, it misses a gain. Avoiding bad days and missing good days are two sides of the same switch. This is why a chart showing only protected crashes would be incomplete evidence. The full path, including quiet periods and rapid recoveries, must be evaluated on dates that were not used to choose the model.

State labels are conventions. In this project, state 0 is favorable and state 1 is unfavorable, while the portfolio position uses 1 for market and 0 for cash. Those numbers point in opposite directions. A state value of 1 therefore leads to a position value of 0. This is a frequent source of bugs, so every later chapter will name both the state and economic position instead of relying on a bare number.

There are four distinct objects in the full chain. First, market observations exist. Second, the model converts past observations into an inferred state. Third, the strategy translates that state into a requested signal. Fourth, execution delay converts the old signal into today's held position. Only the held position earns today's return. Treating those four objects as if they were one column can accidentally give a model money before its signal was available. Chapter 7 will draw the complete daily timeline and show why the distinction changes a backtest.

Layered mathematics

Write the allocation in one equation

Let the starting wealth be multiplied by one plus the portfolio return. The portfolio return is the market return weighted by the market fraction, plus the cash return weighted by the remaining fraction.

W1 = W0 [ w(1+rm) + (1−w) (1+rf) ]
SymbolMeaningUnit in the example
W0Wealth before the periodDollars
W1Wealth after the periodDollars
wFraction allocated to the market, between 0 and 1No unit
rmMarket return over the periodDecimal return
rfCash or risk-free return over the periodDecimal return
Derive the $106.80 result step by step

Use W0=100, w=0.60, rm=0.10, and rf=0.02. The market growth factor is 1.10 and the cash growth factor is 1.02. The weighted growth factor is 0.60×1.10+0.40×1.02=1.068. Multiply $100 by 1.068 to obtain $106.80.

For the paper's binary strategy, w can only be 0 or 1. If w=1, the cash term disappears and ending wealth follows the market. If w=0, the market term disappears and ending wealth follows cash. Later, transaction cost will be subtracted whenever the position changes.

Paper connection

What the paper is testing

Section 3.1 of Shu, Yu, and Mulvey (2024), arXiv:2402.05272v3 defines the regime-switching investment strategy. It allocates 100% to the risky equity index when the inferred regime is favorable and 100% to the local risk-free asset when the regime is unfavorable. The authors deliberately use this simple rule to evaluate the financial value of the regime-identification signal.

The paper compares three routes. Buy-and-hold always keeps position 1. The HMM route converts an HMM state into position 0 or 1. The Jump Model route converts a JM state into position 0 or 1. The paper reports that the JM-guided route improves downside-risk and risk-adjusted metrics across its three markets. That is a claim about the complete protocol, data, timing, costs, and selected models together—not a theorem that every Jump Model must beat buy-and-hold.

Paper statementThe 0/1 strategy is a test vehicle for regime identification. It is not presented as a complete personal investment plan, and this course does not provide investment advice.
Project connection

Where this idea lives in the code

The frozen configuration records signal_risky_value = 1 and signal_cash_value = 0 under [backtest] in research.toml. The accounting implementation is apply_signal() in src/adaptive_jump/backtest.py. Its central calculation is deliberately direct:

gross_return = (
    position * equity_simple
    + (1.0 - position) * cash_return
)

This is the return form of the wealth equation above. The function also validates that positions are binary, applies the execution delay, calculates one-way turnover, subtracts transaction cost, and writes a trade path. A completed run stores those daily columns in each market's sealed trades/*.csv files. The independent verifier recalculates the same accounting instead of trusting the stored metric table.

The short excerpt is not a second implementation. It points to the exact active function. Chapters 7 and 12 will explain why timing and costs must be applied before any strategy comparison.

Evidence and limits

What have we learned—and not learned?

Established by definition

A position of 1 earns the market return. A position of 0 earns the cash return. A mixed position earns a weighted combination before costs.

Not established

We have not shown that a model can identify regimes, that switching improves performance, or that the project's proxy data match the paper.

Scientific boundaryThe allocation lab uses invented returns to teach arithmetic. Moving its sliders is not a backtest, an estimate, a forecast, or evidence for the paper's claim.

A strategy can reduce loss simply by spending less time in the market, but it may also miss gains. A fair evaluation must compare return and risk together, include the cash return, charge trading costs, enforce realistic delay, and measure every competitor on common dates. Those requirements explain why the rest of the course exists.

The binary design is also not a recommendation about personal suitability. A real investor may have taxes, liabilities, a required spending schedule, multiple currencies, restrictions on turnover, or an inability to move an entire portfolio at one closing price. The paper intentionally sets those questions aside to study regime identification. A scientifically useful simplification narrows the question; it does not claim that omitted details are unimportant in every application.

Error diagnosis: “risk-free means no risk exists”

The phrase is a modeling approximation. Short-term government bills may have low default and price risk over the chosen horizon, but inflation, reinvestment, liquidity, currency, measurement, and sovereign risks do not vanish. State the instrument and convention instead of treating “risk-free” as literal perfection.

Advisor explanation

Try first: “The paper uses a deliberately simple switch. In a favorable inferred state, the strategy holds the local equity index; in an unfavorable state, it holds a local short-term cash proxy. This isolates whether regime identification adds economic value before introducing a complicated portfolio optimizer.”

How to explain this to your advisor

Short version: “Every model output eventually becomes one binary economic decision: market or cash. The simplicity is intentional because it lets us test regime identification without hiding the result inside a complex allocation model.”

Important qualification: “A good state label is not enough. The signal must remain useful after causal timing, cash returns, trading delay, costs, and out-of-sample selection.”
